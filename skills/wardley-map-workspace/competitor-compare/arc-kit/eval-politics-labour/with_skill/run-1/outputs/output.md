## Step 2 — Value chain

Three anchors at the top with distinct demands on the organisation:

- **Keir Starmer** drives the government-in-waiting narrative and owns the party's top-of-pyramid brand. Depends on the manifesto, policy framework, and media relations.
- **UK voters** consume public engagement, media coverage, and the policy offer (NHS, education, housing, security). They are the target of polling and targeting.
- **Labour Party members** are the volunteer base, funders, and legitimising body for internal party democracy and the policy framework.

Below the anchors the value chain splits into three functional layers:

1. **Engagement / campaign machinery** (visibility 0.74–0.88): public engagement, campaign strategy, media relations, volunteer mobilisation, fundraising, polling and targeting, stakeholder engagement, private sector collaboration.
2. **Policy apparatus** (visibility 0.66–0.72): party policy framework, internal party democracy, research and policy development, manifesto drafting. This is the connective tissue — it ingests member and stakeholder input and produces the policy offer.
3. **Substantive policy platform** (visibility 0.30–0.62): the 14 policy areas the user listed, positioned by visibility to voters (NHS and anti-discrimination highly visible; digital transformation and infrastructure less directly visible).

Dependency flow: voters → engagement machinery → policy apparatus → substantive policies → (implicit) delivery mechanisms.

## Step 5 — Analysis and recommendations

### The industrialised machinery

The entire **right half of the map** (evolution ≥ 0.55) is industrialised political operations. This is effectively the "shared utility" of UK party politics:

- Media relations, polling, fundraising, volunteer tooling, canvassing databases — Labour buys or configures the same class of products every serious party uses.
- On substantive ground, **national security, anti-discrimination, education access, and NHS framing** have all been moved *into* the industrialised zone — this is partly what "Starmerism" has done since 2020 (detoxifying these areas relative to the Corbyn period).
- *Insight:* Labour's strategic achievement over 2020–2024 has been **commoditising its own brand on previously contested territory**. This is a Brand-and-Marketing / Detoxification play — reducing voter perception risk rather than innovating.

### What is still being invented

The **left half of the map** (evolution ≤ 0.50) is where Labour's actual differentiation and risk both live:

- **New Deal for Working People** (0.30): the single most ambitious legislative undertaking; moves from Custom to Product the moment it becomes statute.
- **GB Energy / climate** (0.28): a brand-new public-sector institution. High execution risk — Genesis-stage institution-building after 25+ years of no equivalent UK precedent.
- **Digital transformation** (0.22): in May 2024 the AI-in-government playbook does not yet exist as a product even globally. Labour will have to build while flying.
- **Government-in-waiting narrative** (0.32): Starmer's personal brand — bespoke, irreplaceable, and the highest-visibility custom component on the map.
- **Private sector collaboration** (0.36): 14 years of opposition have atrophied the muscle. The courtship phase is bespoke.
- **Economic policy frame** (securonomics, 0.38): Reeves's construct is a Custom-Built differentiator — high-visibility for elites, and the frame a Labour government will have to defend.

### Opportunities

- **Productise the New Deal drafting** — treat the legislation as a product-delivery exercise (clear PRD, sprints, legal vendor relationships) rather than bespoke drafting. Moves it from 0.30 to 0.55 faster.
- **Commoditise GB Energy's operational layer** — use existing utility regulation, procurement, and financing stacks; only the *mission* and governance need to be bespoke. Avoid building commodity infrastructure in-house.
- **Industrial-strategy playbook** for private sector collaboration — convert bespoke Starmer-dinner relationships into routinised sector councils with standard terms of reference.
- **Digital transformation** — adopt existing commodity layers aggressively (cloud, off-the-shelf AI) and reserve custom build for the genuinely bespoke parts (legacy HMG data, legislative interfaces).

### Threats

- **Inertia on internal party democracy**: past success at centralising control may block future adaptations the membership will demand in government.
- **Dependency risk (quantified below)**: high-visibility manifesto commitments (NHS, education) depend on Custom-Built fiscal and policy scaffolding that is still being designed.
- **Commodity parity with Conservatives on media/polling**: no differentiation here, only parity.
- **Private sector collaboration** is shallow — if delivery slips in year one, relationship repair will be Genesis-stage all over again.

### Inertia points

| Component | Inertia source |
|---|---|
| Internal party democracy | Leadership prefers current settlement; members may push back in government. |
| National security posture | Consensus is the asset; any evolution risks re-opening Corbyn-era damage. |
| Fundraising | Trade-union legacy structures constrain rapid modernisation. |

### Gameplay patterns at work

- **Brand & Marketing (N)** — the whole Starmer-era repositioning is a textbook play, moving components from Custom/Contested toward Commodity/Consensus.
- **Two-Factor Market / Ecosystem** — voters and private sector both need to be "sold" the same product (stability + investment).
- **Race to Utility** — for government delivery: aggressive consumption of commodity services (cloud, commercial AI) to free budget for the Custom components.
- **Market Enablement (LG)** — GB Energy is a classic market-enablement play: creating a state-anchored actor to unlock a stalled market.
- **Co-evolution with practice** — the New Deal legislation must evolve in step with ACAS, tribunals, and sectoral bargaining practice; a misaligned pace is a failure mode.

### Climatic pressures

- **Everything evolves** — post-election, industrialised components matter less; the Custom ones become the story. Plan capacity accordingly.
- **Higher-order systems create new value** — commoditised engagement machinery frees attention for the Custom components (GB Energy, New Deal).
- **Efficiency enables innovation** — the more ruthlessly campaign ops are commoditised, the more political bandwidth is available for genuinely new policy institutions.

## Summary — the "industrialised vs still being invented" answer

**Industrialised political machinery (right half of map):**
- Media relations, polling, fundraising, volunteer tooling (standard party infrastructure).
- National security posture, anti-discrimination, education access, NHS framing (Starmer-era detoxification has moved these into consensus space).
- Internal party democracy, stakeholder engagement, community programmes (institutional).

**Still being invented (left half of map):**
- The Starmer personal brand and government-in-waiting narrative (Custom, 0.32).
- Workers' rights / New Deal legislation (Custom→Genesis, 0.30).
- GB Energy and climate strategy (Custom→Genesis, 0.28).
- Digital transformation of government (Genesis, 0.22).
- Private sector collaboration at scale (Custom, 0.36).
- The fiscal / securonomics frame (Custom, 0.38).
- Housing delivery at scale (Custom, 0.42).

**Strategic reading:** The campaign is running on commodity rails. The *government* will run on a half-dozen Custom-Built institutions that do not yet exist. Labour's real risk is not the election — it is the first 18 months in office, when three Genesis-stage components (GB Energy, digital government, New Deal bargaining architecture) must simultaneously industrialise while the Starmer narrative carries maximum visibility and cannot fail.
