# Changing Consumer Behaviour and Transport Demand — May 2022

**Anchors:** Government, Transport public, Transport operators (three-anchor map — demand policy, the passenger, and the supplier all drive the value chain independently).
**User need:** Get from A to B, affordably, safely, reliably — increasingly in competition with *not* travelling at all.
**Scope:** National/urban transport demand in the UK/European context, post-COVID, pre-energy-crisis-peak. Covers the user journey, the transport information stack, mode choice, vehicles, connectivity and cyber, city planning, public/private infrastructure down to critical national infrastructure, regulation and sustainability, plus exogenous shocks and virtual-space substitutes.
**Date:** 2022-05.

## Step 2 — Value chain

Four layers, read top-to-bottom (visibility decreasing):

1. **User journey (most visible to the public).** Ease of use, cost, access to service, personal safety, physical safety, awareness. These are what the passenger *experiences*.
2. **Transport information stack.** Transport integration (MaaS), situational services (live arrivals, multi-modal routing), mobility hubs (physical interchange nodes).
3. **Mode choice.** Private mode, shared mode, distributed public mode. Fed by the vehicles (car, train) and the digital substrate (connectivity, cyber).
4. **Infrastructure and policy substrate.** City planning, public infrastructure, private infrastructure, regulation, sustainability/climate. Everything terminates at critical national infrastructure — the rail network, the road network, the power grid that rail increasingly depends on.

Plus two transverse elements:

- **Demand shocks (COVID + Feb 2022 Ukraine fuel-price spike)** — a climatic forcing that reshapes every mode's usage curve simultaneously.
- **Virtual-space alternatives (Zoom/Teams)** — not a transport component, but a substitute that displaces transport demand entirely. Connected to the public and to connectivity.

Two **inertia** (×) flags — these do *not* move:

- **Private mode** — 80+ years of car-centric urban planning, subsidised parking, driver-licensing defaults, and consumer preference mean the "default mode is car" assumption has enormous inertia. Fuel-price shocks move *usage* without moving *ownership*.
- **Public infrastructure and critical national infrastructure** — capital-intensive, ~30-year asset lives, regulated investment cycles. Will not shift position on this map inside the scenario horizon.

### 5.6 Inertia points

| Component | Source | Manifestation |
|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Private mode | Post-war car-centric planning, licensing defaults, tax assumptions | 2022 fuel-price spike reduced car-use but did not reduce car-ownership |
| Car | Installed base, dealer and finance ecosystem | Even with ICE-sales ban in 2030, the fleet half-life pushes effective transition to ~2040 |
| Public infrastructure | 30-year asset lives, regulated investment | You cannot rebuild Network Rail in a product cycle |
| Regulation | Legislative cadence, devolved transport competencies | Transport Bill trailed in May 2022 Queen's Speech but not enacted by year-end |
| City planning | Local plans on 5-15 year cycles; political change | Low-traffic neighbourhood politics shows the friction |

### 5.7 Climatic patterns on the map

- **Everything evolves** — every component is moving except the exogenous shock and the pure inertia points noted above.
- **Past success is a barrier to change** — private-mode inertia; the car is the most entrenched mode precisely because it works.
- **Efficiency enables innovation** — commoditising vehicles, connectivity, and infrastructure is what funds the genesis-stage work (personal safety, hubs, MaaS).
- **Shifts in consumer behaviour reshape the landscape** — COVID forced the virtual-space substitute into Product; fuel prices have forced a re-rating of private-vehicle running costs. These are the observed evolutions that the brief asks about.
- **No choice over evolution (Red Queen)** — transport operators cannot simply decline MaaS or cyber; the passenger will consume via a third-party app regardless.
- **Rates vary by ecosystem** — the passenger-facing digital layer evolves at tech cadence (months); the regulatory/infrastructure layer evolves at political/capital cadence (decades). **This rate mismatch is the core pathology.**

### 5.8 Gameplay patterns worth considering

- **Open approach / standards setting** on MaaS APIs, bus open data, rail data. UK Bus Open Data Service (Jan 2021) is the precedent; extend it.
- **Commoditise via industrialisation** — treat vehicles, connectivity, public infrastructure, CNI as shared utility; focus public investment on the user-journey / info-stack layer.
- **Exploit constraints (climate, cost)** — demand shocks are strategic levers not crises; use fuel-price pain to accelerate mode shift that would have taken years.
- **Defensive regulation (counterplay)** — expect incumbents (car dealers, legacy bus, private rail operating companies) to lobby for status-quo regulation. Track the Transport Bill's passage accordingly.
- **Horizon-3 bet**: the virtual-space alternative is not a transport problem, it is a transport-demand displacer. Planning for capacity that assumes 2019 levels ignores this.

## Recommendations

### Immediate (next 3-6 months)

1. **Separate "demand" from "supply" in every operator metric.** 2022 ridership is still ~80% of 2019 on rail, ~70% on commuter rail in particular, because Zoom has displaced some demand permanently. Capacity decisions made on a 2019 baseline will over-build.
2. **Prioritise personal safety as a first-class outcome.** Highest differentiation pressure on the map; lowest maturity; public scrutiny at its peak post-Everard. Operators with a visible safety-after-dark programme will differentiate.
3. **Commit to mobility-hub pilots with measurable access targets.** The physical hub + MaaS app pairing is the unlock for multimodal journeys; the evidence base is still thin.

### Short-term (6-18 months)

1. **Adopt UNR155/156 and ISO/SAE 21434 as procurement clauses by default.** Transport cybersecurity is the quietest fragility on the map; software-defined vehicles make it critical national dependency risk.
2. **Mandate open data for all bus and shared-mode operators** (build on BODS). Open data is the commoditising wedge that makes Product-stage MaaS possible.
3. **Treat the Transport Bill's post-May 2022 passage as the pacing item** for MaaS, e-scooter legalisation, and CAV governance. Operational capability must be ready the week the bill is enacted.

### Long-term (18+ months)

1. **Plan for a structurally lower but more variable demand baseline.** Virtual-space substitution is permanent for ~10-20% of commuter trips; one-off shocks (energy, climate, pandemic) will add variance of a further 10-20% either way. Rigid schedule models are now brittle.
2. **Shift public capital from vehicle subsidy to hub infrastructure and accessibility**, because the commoditising layer (vehicles) doesn't need subsidy while the Custom-Built hub layer does.
3. **Accept that the regulation/infrastructure axis will always lag the behaviour axis.** Close the gap with adaptive regulation (sandboxes, data-sharing mandates, fast iterative guidance) rather than by trying to make the regulatory cadence match the consumer-behaviour cadence. The gap is the pathology; the gap is also the unavoidable shape of the landscape.

## Summary — the three questions

- **Where is consumer behaviour driving real evolution?** The user-journey layer (ease of use, access, personal safety), the info-stack layer (MaaS, situational services), and mobility hubs. Plus the substitute: Zoom/Teams absorbing commute demand.
- **Where is regulation and infrastructure lagging?** Sustainability measurement (Genesis), regulation of MaaS / e-scooters / CAV (Custom-Built), cybersecurity for software-defined transport (Custom-Built), and the physical hub infrastructure that every MaaS promise depends on.
- **What is the shape of the pathology?** A rate mismatch. The passenger-facing digital layer evolves at tech cadence; the regulatory and infrastructure layers evolve at political and capital cadence. The gap between them is where the fragilities live (ease of use promising integration that MaaS cannot deliver, personal safety promising experience that hubs cannot yet provide, awareness promising carbon data that sustainability measurement doesn't produce). Closing the gap is not realistic; designing around it is the strategy.
