# Wardley Map Analysis: UK Construction Supply Chain (March 2023)

## Purpose

Map the UK construction supply chain end-to-end so that a reader can see at a
glance **what is still bespoke craft and what is ready to be industrialised**.
The map covers demand (government, citizens, mortgage finance), the
building-inspector / QA apparatus, the planning / blueprint / regulation layer,
the labour, equipment, manufacture and materials supply chains, and the newer
modular / prefab / factory-mode delivery routes — together with the two
critical-national-infrastructure (CNI) constraints of local skilled-labour
supply and the material supply chain itself.

Time horizon: March 2023 — post-Brexit, post-COVID, mid-material-cost spike,
pre-the-visible-collapse of several MMC factory ventures (Ilke Homes went into
administration in June 2023; Legal & General Modular Homes closed in late
2023 — in March 2023 these were under visible stress but still operating).

## Key Strategic Observations

1. **The Homes component sits further left than most consumers would guess.**
   It's Product stage, not Commodity — the house you buy is not the house
   your neighbour bought. This is the single biggest industrialisation prize
   on the map, and the reason MMC is a stated government priority.

2. **The value chain contains a genuine bimodal construction mode.** The
   Construction Mode pipeline spans from craft bespoke (Custom-Built, far
   left) all the way to factory-mode MMC (early Product). The gap between
   the two modes is what's being contested in 2023 — and the MMC side is
   losing ground just as the map is drawn (Ilke, L&G Modular under stress).

3. **Two inertia nodes sit below the surface** and bound everything above
   them: Local Skilled-Labour Supply and Material Supply Chain. Both are
   **CNI constraints**: they're relatively low-maturity (Custom-Built
   feeling), low-visibility foundations with hard resistance to
   industrialisation. They will stop any naive "just industrialise it" play
   from the top of the map.

4. **The materials pipeline is the widest evolution span in the chain.**
   From Genesis (novel low-carbon materials) to Commodity (bulk raw
   aggregate). Climate and Net Zero policy is actively pulling Genesis
   components rightward, but the Commodity end is heavily dependent on a
   disrupted supply chain.

5. **The QA apparatus is maturing faster than the construction mode.**
   Post-Grenfell regulatory pressure is dragging inspection toward Commodity
   (Building Safety Regulator, golden-thread requirements under the Building
   Safety Act 2022). But the thing being inspected — bespoke on-site build
   — hasn't moved with it. That mismatch is the crisis Grenfell exposed.

## Doctrine Check

- **Focus on user needs** — Yes: the three anchors (government, citizens,
  mortgage finance) map three distinct user populations with three different
  primary needs (policy outcomes, homes, collateral quality).
- **Know your users** — Government wants stock, citizens want affordability
  and quality, mortgage companies want collateral that holds value. The map
  makes the tension visible: the CNI inertia at the bottom threatens all
  three.
- **Use appropriate methods to stages** — *Failing*: Agile, craft-based
  methods dominate what should be Product-stage delivery. The industry is
  using Custom-Built practice on a component (Homes) that has already
  reached Product maturity. This is a doctrinal mismatch and the single
  biggest root-cause insight from the map.
- **Remove bias and duplication** — The Distributors pipeline and the
  Integrated / Platform distributor end may duplicate effort across
  multiple emerging platforms. A consolidation wave is likely.
- **Manage inertia** — Inertia is explicitly marked (Local Skilled-Labour
  Supply, Material Supply Chain). These are the two components where any
  strategy paper that ignores them will fail.

## Recommended Actions

1. **Build MMC delivery capacity as a Town-Planner activity, not a
   Pioneer bet** (horizon 12–24 months). The pipeline shows Factory
   Manufacture is sitting at Custom-Built and visibly under-capitalised.
   Treat it as a utility to be built (state-backed demand-aggregation,
   long-run offtake agreements with housing associations), not a
   venture-capital bet on individual factory operators.

2. **Treat Local Skilled-Labour Supply as a 10-year CNI investment.**
   Apprenticeships, T-levels, and retention pay are levers; immigration
   policy is a lever the industry doesn't own. Any industrialisation plan
   that assumes elastic labour supply will fail inside 24 months.

3. **Stress-test the Material Supply Chain for sovereignty.** Cement,
   steel, plasterboard, timber — identify single-source geographic
   dependencies and build second sources. This is a classic supplier-
   inertia problem and should be treated as such.

4. **Accelerate the Commodity end of Building Inspection / QA.** The
   Building Safety Regulator regime is there; the bottleneck is
   inspector supply and digital golden-thread tooling. Invest in the
   tooling, not new process.

5. **Separate the Homes product from the Homes build.** Productise the
   home (standardised design, pattern-book, type-approved modules) while
   letting the build mode evolve underneath. This is the "buy the product,
   outsource the delivery" pattern — exactly what the Construction Mode
   pipeline invites.

## Build vs. Buy Assessment

| Component | Recommendation | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Factory Manufacture (modules) | **Build / Invest** | Custom-Built and thin — UK strategic capacity is a sovereignty issue; market alone is failing to fund it. |
| Novel / low-carbon materials (left end of materials pipeline) | **Build / Invest** | Genesis; competitive advantage is still up for grabs; policy-pulled. |
| Local Skilled-Labour Supply | **Build** (long-term) | Custom-Built with inertia; cannot be bought. |
| Raw materials (cement, aggregate, steel) | **Buy** | Commodity; focus on diversifying source, not on producing. |
| Equipment & Plant Hire | **Buy / Rent** | Commodity-utility market — never build your own digger fleet. |
| Mortgage Companies | **Buy (partner)** | Commodity-utility finance — no strategic advantage in integrating finance. |
| Building Inspection / QA | **Outsource** | Regulated service with competitive market of approved inspectors. |
| Logistics / Freight | **Buy** | Commodity. |
| Traditional Distributors (right end of distributor pipeline) | **Buy** | Product; mature vendor market. |
| Platform Distributors (left end) | **Partner / Invest** | Custom-Built emerging; early mover advantage still exists. |

## Evolution Predictions (12–24 months)

- **Construction Mode** will drift rightward as MMC survivors consolidate
  after the 2023 shakeout — but slower than policy wants. The early MMC
  factories are dying; the survivors will mature.
- **Factory Manufacture** will split: the weak operators die, the survivors
  (those with captive offtake, typically housing associations) move toward
  Product.
- **Building Materials**: the Commodity end stays put, the Genesis end
  (low-carbon, CLT) accelerates rightward under Net Zero policy and
  embodied-carbon regulation.
- **Distributors**: platform distributors will compress the traditional
  merchant margin; expect consolidation.
- **Architecture / Blueprints**: continued BIM rightward drift, plus early
  generative-design / AI-assisted blueprint tooling (Genesis additions to
  the pipeline, not yet visible at the map's scale).
- **Inertia components (Local Skilled-Labour Supply, Material Supply
  Chain)** will NOT move. They are the hard constraints. Any forecast that
  assumes they will is suspect.

## Open Questions

1. **Who is the anchor for public-sector new-build?** Homes England?
   Individual housing associations? The map treats Government as a
   unitary anchor, but the purchaser of social housing is highly
   fragmented — a submap may be warranted.
2. **Is Retrofit a separate map?** The scenario covers new-build
   construction; retrofit (insulation, heat pumps, EWI) has a different
   evolution profile and a different labour pool. Strongly consider a
   second map.
3. **How should Commercial construction sit against Residential?**
   Commercial (offices, warehouses, logistics sheds) is further along
   on industrialisation than residential (especially warehouses). The
   current map averages both; a split may reveal more.
