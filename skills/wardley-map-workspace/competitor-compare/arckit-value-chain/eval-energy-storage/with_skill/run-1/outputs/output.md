# Value Chain — Electrical Energy Storage Landscape (April 2023)

Generated by: arckit-wardley.value-chain (partial-map skill; ε placeholder = 0.50)
Scenario: map the landscape of electrical energy storage connecting consumers, activity, control, electricity generation, and the grid — covering traditional vs distributed (TCP/IP-style fault-tolerant) grid models, AGC / SCADA / spinning reserve / fast-response, storage types (electrical, hydro, hydrogen), the energy platform and its integration layer (pre-approval/discovery, adaptive, plug-and-play), and the chain underneath (sources, stockpile, material supply chain, supply-chain awareness).

## 1. Executive Summary

Anchor: Consumer can run activity on reliable electricity mediated by grid control. 23 components identified across 8 visibility levels. Two parallel control spines descend from Control: a **traditional** spine (Traditional Grid Model → Grid, SCADA, Spinning Reserve) modelling the grid as a single machine, and a **distributed** spine (Distributed Grid Model → Grid, Fast-Response Systems, Energy Platform) modelling it as a fault-tolerant TCP/IP-style network. Storage joins the chain twice: directly beneath Fast-Response Systems (electrical/battery, for sub-second response), and beneath the Energy Platform (electrical, hydro, hydrogen — arbitraged through the integration layer). The chain terminates on a deep material substrate (Sources → Stockpile → Material Supply Chain → Awareness) that is shared by all three storage types and by generation itself — this is the pinch point where material supply chain risk bites.

## 6. Critical Path Analysis

- **Traditional critical path**: Consumer → Activity → Control → Traditional Grid Model → Grid → Electricity Generation → Sources → Stockpile → Material Supply Chain → Awareness of Energy Supply Chain.
- **Distributed critical path**: Consumer → Activity → Control → Distributed Grid Model → Energy Platform → Integration Layer → (Pre-approval / Discovery ∧ Adaptive Integration ∧ Plug-and-Play) with storage fanning out to Electrical / Hydro / Hydrogen → Sources / Material Supply Chain.
- **Fast-response critical path**: Control → Distributed Grid Model → Fast-Response Systems → Electrical Storage → Material Supply Chain. This path has the fewest hops and the tightest time constant (sub-second), but terminates on the most constrained substrate (battery materials).
- **Bottlenecks / single points of failure**:
  - **Grid** is the single physical node both grid models fall back on — a physical grid failure cannot be routed around by any model.
  - **SCADA** is the only telemetry substrate for both the Traditional Grid Model and AGC; a SCADA compromise affects both spines simultaneously.
  - **Material Supply Chain** is a choke point shared by Electrical Storage, Hydrogen Storage, and (via Sources) all generation. This is where supply-chain risk bites the hardest.
  - **Energy Platform** is a concentration point for all three storage types and is the only path the Distributed Grid Model uses to reach storage.
- **Resilience gaps**: The Traditional Grid Model has no storage path other than Spinning Reserve (which is itself generation, not storage). If battery-material supply tightens, the distributed spine — which relies on Electrical Storage via both Fast-Response Systems and the Energy Platform — is disproportionately exposed. Pumped-hydro (Hydro Storage) is the only storage capability with material-supply-chain resilience, but depends on siting (Sources).

### Strategic observations requested by the scenario

- **Distributed fault-tolerant grids evolving faster than traditional control**: Both grid-model siblings sit at the same visibility (0.72) but descend into very different substrates. The Distributed Grid Model depends on the Energy Platform + Integration Layer + Fast-Response Systems stack — three components whose lower-visibility position indicates they are earlier in evolution (note: ε is set to the 0.50 placeholder at the value-chain stage; this is a structural observation about distance from anchor, not a cheat-sheet score). The Traditional Grid Model depends on a more mature SCADA + Spinning Reserve stack. The structural asymmetry the chain exposes is: the distributed spine is the one accumulating new components (platform, integration, plug-and-play) while the traditional spine is static; that is where evolutionary pressure is concentrated.
- **Where material supply chain risk bites**: The chain reveals three convergent paths into Material Supply Chain — via Electrical Storage (batteries), via Hydrogen Storage (electrolyser / fuel-cell materials), and via Sources (fuels and generation inputs). Hydro Storage is the only storage branch that does not transit Material Supply Chain, and Awareness of Energy Supply Chain is the deepest commodity in the chain — the chain shows it as infrastructure even though it is treated as a capability need in the scenario. This placement (0.07) argues for promoting Awareness higher in the chain in any follow-up mapping iteration, because underinvestment in awareness is what makes the material risk invisible until it bites.

## 9. Assumptions and Open Questions

Assumptions:

- **Consumer** is the primary user and therefore the anchor; grid operators, asset owners, and regulators operate the chain rather than sit on it.
- **Traditional Grid Model** and **Distributed Grid Model** are siblings at the same visibility — both are worldviews over the same physical Grid, not separate physical grids.
- **Spinning Reserve** is a *child* of the Traditional Grid Model and of AGC (it is the traditional response mechanism); it is not hung off Generation directly because the scenario asks about it as a grid-control primitive.
- **Fast-Response Systems** are a *child* of the Distributed Grid Model and of AGC; they are the modern counterpart to Spinning Reserve, buffered by Electrical Storage.
- **Pre-approval / Discovery**, **Adaptive Integration**, and **Plug-and-Play** are three **capabilities** of the Integration Layer, not three different integration layers. They are modelled as siblings under Integration Layer.
- **Electrical Storage** has two parents — Fast-Response Systems (sub-second path) and Energy Platform (market path) — because both genuinely depend on it and removing either edge would misrepresent how storage participates in the grid.
- **Hydro Storage** does not depend on Material Supply Chain (civil works depend on cement and steel, but the dominant dependency is on Sources / siting); this is a deliberate simplification to highlight the material-risk asymmetry between electrochemical and potential-energy storage.
- **Awareness of Energy Supply Chain** is placed deepest because the scenario frames it as a foundational capability that all upstream components depend on when disruption hits.

Open questions:

- Should the two grid models be separated further — e.g., should the Distributed Grid Model connect directly to Storage, bypassing the Energy Platform, to represent peer-to-peer energy markets?
- Is **Integration Layer** a single component with three capabilities, or three parallel integration modes that different asset classes use (batteries → plug-and-play; hydrogen → pre-approval; hydro → adaptive)?
- Should **Hydro Storage** depend on Material Supply Chain via civil-works materials (cement, steel), or is that noise at this abstraction level?
- Does **Awareness of Energy Supply Chain** belong as deep infrastructure (as drawn, 0.07) or as a user-facing dashboard capability sitting nearer the operator (higher up the chain)? Both readings are defensible.
- Should **SCADA** and **AGC** be split by generation-side vs grid-side scope? The scenario treats them as unified.
